Longitudinal vs. Transverse Waves

The imaging source stays still in these diagrams. What oscillates is the medium for ultrasound, and the electromagnetic field for X-rays.

Longitudinal Wave: Ultrasound

Particle motion is parallel to the direction the wave travels.

🔊
stationary
ultrasound probe
medium particles wiggle left ↔ right
👂

being imaged
Key idea: The ultrasound probe emits pressure waves. Nearby particles in tissue move back and forth, creating alternating regions of compression and rarefaction.

Transverse Wave: X-rays

Field oscillation is perpendicular to the direction the wave travels.

⚡
stationary
X-ray tube/source
electromagnetic field oscillates up ↕ down
🩻
object / detector
X-ray image
Key idea: The X-ray tube emits electromagnetic radiation. The tube itself is not moving up and down; the oscillating quantity is the electromagnetic field.
Teaching note: The icons are simplified imaging examples. Ultrasound is a mechanical wave that requires a medium. X-rays are electromagnetic waves and do not require a material medium to travel.
